An Article on
Excessive Campaign Spending

By: Bryan James A. Pidazo | 2019 May 12

This is important? Good question. I asked myself the same thing, but yes this does matter and I'll go over why that is. First of all, why does excessive spending matter? There is a limit set for each candidate to ensure that there is an even playing field among the political parties and candidates as well as penalties for overspending for the ad campaigns. It has, however, been revealed that candidates have been secretly spending much more than they are expected to be, with some candidates spending well over their own net-worth (Mangahas, 2019).

One thing to take away from this is not only are some candidates are spending more than others but also the source of where these candidates acquired such a large sum to begin with. It was suggested that the resources from the pre-campaign ads were either hidden wealth or that these were donations to the candidate while in public office. If the latter were true, then such actions can be considered as a form of bribery which, at the moment, we have no means of determining.
Then again, the cause for why candidates overspend during the pre-campaign may be justified for the reason that the given spending limit is too low for the funding of resources. With citizens stating that the existing 25 year-old rates are no longer realistic for today's standard (Rappler, 2019). Granted, in 2018, a bill regarding the increase of candidates' spending limit was pushed by the senate (Felipe, 2018). The bill notes that although the amount is higher, it remains "conservative.", with the amounts of the President and Vice President remaining at P10 per voter. Committee Report 497 showed that independent senatorial, party-list and other candidates may spend P6 to P8 per voter while candidates running under a political party or are being endorsed by political parties may spend P6 per voter. It added that the existing cap of P5 for political parties has also been increased to P8 per voter.

Although said increase was meant to discourage overspending by candidates, the recent election campaign still reported the amount of spending during the pre-campaigning was definitely over the limit. With everything I have mentioned, it's safe to say that candidates aren't exactly spending excessively on the campaign, but rather the set limit for such is quite low for the candidates' standards. Given that I gave some explanation of why this matters, at the end of the day, one can only hope for luck after this year's election.

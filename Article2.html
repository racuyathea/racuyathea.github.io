An Article on People Choosing Incompetent Candidates

By: Bryan James A. Pidazo | 2019 May 12

Under democracy, it is up to a country's citizens to decide which individuals are qualified to be elected as leaders of the country (Lozada, 2013). The idea is that people are given the power to be directly responsible for who runs their country, this makes it so that citizens are free to choose the candidate who they trust and who they believe are qualified to be elected. Unfortunately, in the Philippine government, it is the corrupt and incompetent who end up within its court, but how come? It was the voters who elected these individuals to be responsible for their country, but why? Why do they constantly give away their very important votes to corrupt politicians and officials? There are several reasons for that but let's begin with the ignorance of the voters within the country.

Okay, 'ignorance' may be too strong a word, but before anything one should first understand the requirements to be an eligible voter. First, they must be at least 18 years of age, and a resident of the Philippines for at least 1 year and in the place where in he/she proposes to vote, for at least 6 months immediately preceding the elections.
Disqualifications for registry are also as follows: Any person who has been sentenced by final judgment to suffer imprisonment for not less than 1 year, any person who has been adjudged by final judgment by a competent court or tribunal of having committed rebellion, insurrection, violation of the firearms laws, or any crime against national security unless restored to his (or her) full civil and political rights in accordance with law and finally, any insane or incompetent person as declared by competent authority (COMELEC, n.d.).
Now that's basically it, no education required, just be 18, don't be insane and you are now responsible for the selection of your country's leaders for the next several years. The obvious issue with that is that the lower class, which make up the majority of the Philippine population, who are unable to acquire educational opportunities or have never even enrolled in education at all are eligible voters in the election. It's these people, who are easily swayed by running candidates that decide who gets to run their government.

Another thing worth noting is that these people don't have the resources to acquire the information they need to understand how qualified a candidate actually is (Sarmiento, 2013). All they need to know about someone is how selfish they appear to be. Candidates who are shown to be helpful to poor communities and offer free education to lucky families get the obvious upper hand in votes compared to candidates who provide effective and needed platforms because but have less advertising campaigns. 'Why choose someone I don't recognize when a popular celebrity who gives free education is running for the same position?' is the thought that unfortunately, lower class voters follow.
Still, it's not their fault that they don't know. Some of these advertising campaigns can be very manipulative and targeted specifically towards that lower class of voters (Avila, 2015). Candidates know that people like that sort of thing and they exploit it as a means of getting the advantage in the polls, and sadly that's just the way things are. Additionally, not all voters are uneducated. Middle and high class citizens are also eligible for voting too, but based on the individuals who have been running for government positions in the past years, middle class voting probably just goes along the lines of voting for the candidate that's less evil than the other, and this is quite the terrible truth of the Philippine elections.
